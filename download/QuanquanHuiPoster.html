Background

The term "single transit" or "mono transit" refers to the occurrence of an exoplanet transit appearing only once in an entire light curve. Although the transit method has become the most widely used technique for detecting exoplanets, single transits have not received significant attention. Despite various methods being employed to detect transit signals, there is a notable lack of effective automated detection methods for single transits. Traditional manual detection methods are clearly unsuitable for the current era of large-scale astronomical data. This work proposes a novel automated method for detecting single transits in light curves.

Method

For detecting general periodic transits in the raw light curve, the most commonly used methods are Box Least Squares (BLS) [3] and Transit Least Squares (TLS) [4]. The basic principles of both methods are the same. They use an ideal transit light curve and compare it with the folded raw light curve at different periods, processing the difference between them to determine the positions and confidence levels of the transits present in the raw light curve. The search for periodic transits is conducted in the parameter space with three dimensions: T0 (epoch time), duration, and period. The folded light curve is used to improve the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) and detection efficiency.

We use a least square method similar to BLS and TLS. Since a single transit shows only once in the light curve, folding the light curve to improve SNR and detection efficiency used in  BLS and TLS is not part of our algorithm.

Specifically, the search for single transits will be conducted in the parameter space with two dimensions: T0 (epoch time) and duration. Glance generates ideal transit light curves based on a predetermined grid and comapares with the actual light curves to search for possible single transit events.

The method runs on a GPU, and thanks to the high degree of parallelization offered by the GPU, the time taken to analyze each light curve is less than one second. Currently, no publicly available systematic method for detecting single transits has been identified for comparison.

Simulation

To evaluate the impact of white noise on the Glance method, we conducted tests using a fixed 2300 ppm single transit signal in light curves spanning one TESS sector (approximately 28 days), with varying levels of white noise. The 2300 ppm is the average depth of all possible single transits from TESS data on the ExoFop website*.

Since the most common metric for evaluating the accuracy of transit searches, the period, is not applicable to single transits, we employ the difference in T0 (epoch time) to assess the accuracy of single transit detection. In our simulations, we consider the recovery to be accurate when the T0 Loss is less than 0.05.

Thus, we define precision as the ratio of the number of accurately recovered simulation targets to the total number of simulation targets.

Abstract

There is a significant gap between the estimates of the number of single transits in TESS data and the actual number of detections. In contrast to methods such as BLS and TLS, which are designed to detect periodic transits, there is a lack of a systematic approach for detecting and validating single transits. In this work, a novel single transit detection method is designed and developed. The method involves comparing light curves generated by the Batman program with actual light curves and uses the least squares method to filter out potential single transit positions. Unlike the BLS and TLS methods, this method does not require folding of light curves and can run efficiently on GPU. This method has successfully detected 62.7% targets of 59 known single transits in the TESS data within a 0.1-day epoch time error margin and identified 20 new single transit candidates. This new method and planet candidates will be reported.
